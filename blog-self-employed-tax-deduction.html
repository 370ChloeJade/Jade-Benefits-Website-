Self-Employed Health Insurance Tax Deduction: The Complete Guide

By Jade Benefits | Fort Lauderdale, FL | Updated July 2026

If you are self-employed and paying for your own health insurance, there is a good chance you are entitled to write off every dollar of premium you pay, without ever itemizing. It is one of the most overlooked deductions for 1099 workers and small business owners, mostly because the rules around who qualifies and how much you can claim are not well explained. Here is how it actually works.

What the deduction actually is

The self-employed health insurance deduction lets you deduct what you pay in premiums directly from your income, before you get to adjusted gross income. That makes it an above-the-line deduction, which means two things in practice: you do not need to itemize to claim it, and you skip the 7.5% of AGI threshold that limits the medical expense deduction on Schedule A for everyone else.

Who qualifies

You can generally claim the deduction if you are one of the following:

  • A sole proprietor reporting net profit on Schedule C
  • A partner receiving guaranteed payments or a distributive share of partnership income
  • An LLC member taxed as a sole proprietorship or partnership
  • An S corporation shareholder who owns more than 2% of the company's stock, under special rules described below

One rule trips up a lot of people: if you were eligible to be covered under a spouse's employer-subsidized plan for part of the year, you can only deduct premiums for the months you were not eligible for that coverage. If your spouse's plan covered you starting in July, for example, you can only deduct premiums for January through June.

What it covers

The deduction is not limited to a single medical plan. You can generally include:

  • Medical insurance premiums
  • Dental and vision insurance premiums
  • Qualified long-term care insurance, up to age-based limits
  • Coverage for your spouse and dependents
  • Coverage for a child who was under age 27 at year end, even if that child was not your tax dependent

Quick tip: Because the deduction is not capped at a flat dollar amount, someone paying $18,000 a year in premiums with $60,000 in net profit can deduct the full $18,000, not just a portion of it.

The limits you need to know

Two limits matter most:

  • Net profit limit: your deduction cannot exceed the net self-employment income from the specific business under which the health plan is established. If your business had a loss for the year, you generally cannot claim the deduction that year.
  • Other coverage limit: you cannot deduct premiums for months you were eligible to participate in an employer-subsidized health plan, whether through your own job, your spouse's job, or a dependent's employer.

It is also worth knowing what the deduction does not do: it lowers your income tax, but it does not reduce the net earnings figure used to calculate self-employment tax (Social Security and Medicare). That is a common point of confusion.

How to claim it

The deduction is calculated on Form 7206 (Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction), and the resulting amount flows to Schedule 1 (Form 1040), line 17, as an above-the-line adjustment to income. S corporation shareholders follow a slightly different path: the corporation includes the premium amount on the shareholder's W-2, and the shareholder then claims the deduction on Schedule 1 using the amount reported.

Common mistakes

The most frequent errors are claiming the full annual premium when you had employer-subsidized coverage available for part of the year, forgetting that an S corp shareholder's premiums must run through payroll and appear on the W-2 to qualify, and assuming the deduction reduces self-employment tax when it only reduces income tax.

Frequently asked questions

Can self-employed people deduct health insurance premiums?

Yes. Sole proprietors, partners, LLC members, and more-than-2% S corporation shareholders can generally deduct premiums for medical, dental, vision, and qualified long-term care insurance for themselves, their spouse, and their dependents, as an above-the-line deduction that does not require itemizing.

Is there a dollar limit on the self-employed health insurance deduction?

There is no fixed dollar cap, but the deduction cannot exceed your net self-employment income from the specific business under which the insurance plan is established.

Does the self-employed health insurance deduction reduce self-employment tax?

No. It reduces your income tax by lowering adjusted gross income, but it does not reduce the net earnings figure used to calculate self-employment tax.

What form do I use to claim it?

Form 7206 calculates the deduction, and the result is reported on Schedule 1 (Form 1040), line 17.
